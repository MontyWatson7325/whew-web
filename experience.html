Where they were

Rotan, Fisher County, Texas. October 1918.

Your great-grandfather Enos Watson had been in Fisher County for twenty years. He came west from the older settlements, broke ground, survived the droughts, raised a family. His son Morris — nineteen years old — was studying to be a lawyer.

Fisher County, West Texas · 1918

What they faced

In October 1918 the Spanish flu hit Rotan. It came on the train from Abilene. By the second week of October it had moved through three families on the east side of town. Morris was running a fever. Enos sent for the doctor — twenty miles away, already overrun. He waited.

Morris's fever broke on its own. The way the flu either broke or it didn't.

Rotan lost twenty-six people that fall. The Watson family was not among them.

How they came through it

Twenty years later, Morris Watson was practicing law in Fisher and Scurry County. The son of a farmer, the only lawyer for forty miles. That didn't happen by accident. It happened because Enos Watson held on when the rest of the world was falling apart, and Morris Watson survived a plague at nineteen and decided to build something anyway.

That persistence runs in your line.

Where they were

Southern Alabama. May 1865.

Your great-great-grandfather was a private in the 6th Alabama Cavalry. When the surrender terms were read aloud to the regiment, fewer than two hundred men were left standing — out of nearly a thousand who had mustered in 1863. He was one of them. He had been fighting for two years: through the Atlanta Campaign, back south to Florida, through skirmishes across central Alabama. He is handed his parole papers. He is told he may go home.

6th Alabama Cavalry · Alabama, May 1865

What they faced

Home is a hundred miles north and west with no rail line running and no army left to feed him. He fought for the Confederacy — that was wrong, and history settled that. What it doesn't record is this: he is twenty-something years old, thin as wire, and the road home runs through burned bridges, collapsed fences, and fields that haven't been planted in two years.

He walks for weeks. Blisters heal and re-open. He eats what he finds. He passes through communities where every family has lost someone, where grief sits in the air like weather. He passes through communities where the men didn't come back at all.

How they came through it

He comes home to a farm that is still standing. Not everyone does. He puts in a crop. It comes in. He has children. One of those children's children moves west — to Texas, to the flat country beyond the Brazos — and the line continues until it reaches you.

The walk home was not heroic. There was no glory in it. He was a defeated man walking a broken road back to a changed world, carrying nothing but his parole papers and whatever was left of him. That is exactly what it took to keep the line going.

That stubbornness — putting one foot in front of the other when there is every reason to stop — runs in your line.
